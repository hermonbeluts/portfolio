Causal Inference Sleep Study

Analyzing the Effect of Sleep on Student Exam Performance | Fall 2024

Project Overview

The goal of this project was to choose a topic and conduct a causal inference analysis determining whether one variable causes the outcome. We specifically explored the causal relationship between sleep duration and exam performance. Using a Kaggle dataset containing various features related to student performance, we examined whether sleeping more than six hours per night (which we defined as the benchmark for "adequate sleep") impacts exam scores.

Process

The project involved several steps:

  • Research Design: Sourcing relevant dataset, drafting the causal research question and potential outcomes notation, and identifying relevant variables using a Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) to determine the sufficient adjustment set.
  • Data Cleaning: Processed the dataset to handle missing values, standardize variables, and dichotomize sleep hours into adequate vs. inadequate.
  • Analysis: Used Propensity Score Matching to estimate the Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT).
  • Results: Found that adequate sleep is associated with a negligible decrease in exam scores, suggesting no significant effect.

Key Visualizations

Key Findings

Based on our results, we concluded that:

  • The estimated ATT is -0.0374, meaning students with adequate sleep scored 0.0374 standard deviations lower than those with inadequate sleep.
  • The small effect size suggests sleep duration may not significantly impact exam performance.
  • Results emphasize the importance of considering other factors like sleep quality, stress, and socioeconomic status.
